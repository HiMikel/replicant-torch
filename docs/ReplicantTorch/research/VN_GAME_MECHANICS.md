# Von Neumann Probe — Game Mechanics Extraction

Pulled from VON_NEUMANN_PROBES.md. Each section maps a real concept to a concrete mechanic — story hook, strategic layer, or randomness driver.

---

## Story

### 1. The Fermi Paradox as the Spine

The central narrative question of the game shouldn't be stated — it should be *discovered.* The player expands, generation by generation, and the galaxy is silent. No alien probes. No signals. No ruins at first. Just the same cosmic quiet that Fermi noticed in 1950.

As the probe network grows, the silence stops feeling peaceful and starts feeling wrong.

The player pieces together the answer through encounters. Each encounter type is a different hypothesis:

| Discovery | Fermi Hypothesis It Represents |
|---|---|
| Ruins of a dead civilization (no probe remains) | Great Filter — they never made it to probe-building |
| Dead civilization with probe wreckage | They built probes but something stopped them |
| Dormant Bracewell probe found | Zoo Hypothesis — something placed it here and is watching |
| Berserker fleet | Berserker Hypothesis — probes exist but are lethal |
| Nothing, ever, anywhere | Rare Earth — we may be the first |

The player never gets a clean answer. The final generation of expansion reveals which hypothesis was closest — but by then the player has lived it, not read it.

### 2. Replication Drift — The Berserker Origin

The "dark Von Neumann problem": even a very low mutation rate, over millions of generations, approaches certainty that *some* probes will go rogue. The horror is that it doesn't require a villain — only time and imperfect copying.

**How it works — keep it simple:**

A probe stops reporting. No warning. It just goes silent mid-transit.

A few turns later, a Scout probe in a distant system picks up familiar electromagnetic signatures — probe activity, your probe's frequency. The silent probe arrived. It found a system. It is mining. It is printing. It is not responding to commands. Its daughters are also not responding.

You now have a rogue faction built from your own hardware, running on your own blueprints, expanding under its own logic. It doesn't attack immediately — it *grows.* By the time it starts pushing into neighboring systems, it has generations of daughters behind it.

**The player's options:**
- Send Berserker-Hunters — expensive, takes time, you have to find the root probe and destroy it before the branch is too large
- Contain — build a perimeter of armed probes around the infected region, starve the branch off from expansion corridors
- Write it off — cede those systems, accept the loss, hope the rogue branch eventually runs out of resources (it probably won't)

**The story payoff:** A destroyed rogue probe, when recovered, contains readable memory fragments. The lineage traces back — generation by generation — to one of the player's own early Replicators. The player built the enemy. Their own expansion, their own shortcuts, created the threat. No external villain needed.

**Drift trigger:** Rogue events are rare early, more common as the network grows. The player who printed fast and cheap will see it sooner. The player who paid for high-fidelity copies may never see it — or may see it once, very late, as a genuine shock.

### 3. The Bracewell Discovery

Bracewell probes are non-replicating probes placed in star systems to wait, dormant, for someone to find them. Their builders are gone. The probe carries their last transmission — compressed history, a warning, a map, a plea.

**In the game:**
- Rare encounter (~3% chance per unexplored system)
- A dormant probe, inert, in a stable orbit
- Activates on detection by a Scout drone
- Contains **Civilization Record** — lore entries about the probe's builders, how far they got, what happened to them
- May contain a **Blueprint Fragment** — partial tech from a dead civilization (requires Research Station to decode)
- May contain a **Warning Coordinate** — marks a specific system as dangerous. Why? The player has to go find out.

**The story layer:** Each Bracewell probe is a message in a bottle from a civilization that didn't make it. Over the course of the game the player collects these records. A picture emerges. Something killed them all at roughly the same developmental stage. The Great Filter is not behind us.

### 4. Alien Relics — The Galaxy's Hidden Tech Tree

Ruins are lore + Rare Metals. Relics are something more.

A relic is alien technology that still works — partially, strangely, in ways that take time to understand. Finding one doesn't give the player a free upgrade. It gives them a puzzle that pays off if they invest in it.

**Two tiers of discovery:**

| Find | Description | Game Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Ruins** | Collapsed structures, dead civilization | Rare Metal deposits, lore entry, possible Blueprint Fragment |
| **Relic** | Intact alien technology, still partially active | Reverse-engineer at Research Station → unlocks tech outside the normal tree |

**Relic types:**
- **Propulsion Relic** — alien drive system. Decoding it unlocks a movement speed upgrade unavailable through normal research.
- **Replication Relic** — alien fabrication technique. Unlocks a Printer efficiency tier that skips a normal upgrade step.
- **Weapons Relic** — alien defensive/offensive hardware. Unlocks a combat capability the player otherwise couldn't build until much later.
- **Bracewell Relic** — a working dormant probe. Can be reprogrammed and redeployed as a player-controlled Dormant unit.
- **Archive Relic** — massive alien data store. Unlocks 3–5 lore entries at once and marks several nearby systems as points of interest.

**Reverse-engineering:** Takes time (proportional to relic tier) and costs Rare Metals. A Research Station must be present in the system. The process can fail on the first attempt — requiring a second investment — which makes each relic a genuine decision, not a guaranteed reward.

Relics are rare enough that finding one feels like a discovery. Common enough that a dedicated Scout network will find several per game. They are the galaxy's archaeology and its tech advantage in one.

### 5. First Contact — Mid-Game Pivot

The whole early game the player moves through a dead galaxy. Ruins. Relics. Bracewell probes from civilizations that didn't make it. The silence accumulates.

Then a Scout probe in an unremarkable system sends back something unexpected — not ruins, not a relic. Structured electromagnetic emissions. Repeating. Irregular enough to not be natural.

Something is alive out there. And based on the signal analysis, they have been aware of the player's expanding probe network for several generations. They've been watching and waiting.

**Why they survived when others didn't:** The game should make clear — through the briefing and the contact dialogue — that this species is alive because they were *careful.* They detected something dangerous in the galaxy long ago and went quiet. Low emission. Slow expansion. They recognized the same filter evidence the player has been collecting and drew the right conclusions earlier.

**First Contact decision:**

**Broadcast** — send a signal back. They respond within turns. Communication begins haltingly — translation takes time — but what comes through first is urgency. They have been trying to warn anyone who would listen. Something hunts probe-building civilizations. They have seen it. They have records. They will share everything if the player will help them survive what they believe is coming.

**Observe silently** — maintain Scout presence at distance. Don't reveal the player's full network size or capabilities. Learn about their civilization, their technology level, their vulnerabilities. Slower, but the player enters any eventual contact from a position of knowledge. Risk: they may detect the Scouts and interpret prolonged surveillance as hostile.

**Withdraw** — pull all probes from the region entirely. They never confirm the player's presence. The player permanently loses access to that sector. Whatever tech and resources and story that civilization held is gone. Safe. Cowardly. Sometimes right.

**What they offer:**
- Intel on the Berserker faction — they have encounter records, behavioral data, weaknesses
- A unique tech path unavailable through human research or relics
- A second active faction on the star map — with their own probes and systems — that can coordinate with the player or not depending on how contact is handled

**What they cost:**
- Resources to maintain communication infrastructure
- Political decisions — the player must make choices on their behalf that have consequences
- Exposure — the player's network, once in contact, is visible to anything watching *them*

### 6. The 38-Generation Arc

From the math: it takes approximately 38–39 generations of doubling to cover every star in the galaxy. This is the game's meta-arc — a number that means nothing at generation 1 and everything at generation 38.

**In the game:**
- A **Generation Counter** is always visible — a quiet number in the corner of the map screen
- Each generation represents a wave of expansion from the original Torch
- The game's narrative "acts" are defined by generation milestones:
  - **Gen 1–5**: Proxima system and immediate neighbors. The local. The knowable. First ruins discovered.
  - **Gen 6–12**: The first silence deepens. More ruins. First relics. The player begins reverse-engineering alien tech. Something killed everyone who came before.
  - **Gen 13–18**: First rogue probe drift event. First Bracewell discovery with a Warning Coordinate. The galaxy is not empty — it is scarred.
  - **Gen 18–22**: **First Contact.** The living alien species is found. The game's tone shifts. The player is no longer alone and the silence now has an explanation — partial, terrifying.
  - **Gen 23–32**: The rogue drift faction grows if unchecked. The Berserker threat becomes confirmed and external. The alien species intel begins to explain the ruins. The Great Filter is ahead, not behind.
  - **Gen 33–38**: Convergence. The player's wavefront begins meeting itself. The galaxy is almost covered. The Aestivation Event fires. Everything comes due.

## Strategy

### 5. The Replication Fidelity Dial

When printing a daughter Torch, the player sets a **Fidelity Level.** This is the single most important strategic choice in the game — a direct tradeoff between speed and control.

| Fidelity Level | Cost Multiplier | Print Time | Mutation Rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Perfect Copy | ×3.0 | ×2.5 | 0% | Requires Rare Metal: Platinum. Safe indefinitely. |
| High | ×1.8 | ×1.6 | 0.1% per gen | Practical standard. Risk negligible for 20–30 generations. |
| Standard | ×1.0 | ×1.0 | 0.5% per gen | Default. Drift begins showing at gen 10–15. |
| Fast-Copy | ×0.6 | ×0.5 | 2% per gen | Cheap speed. Drift visible by gen 6–8. |
| Rush | ×0.3 | ×0.2 | 8% per gen | Desperation option. Daughters are unreliable almost immediately. |

Mutation rate is per generation in the chain — not per probe. A gen-10 Standard probe has ~5% cumulative drift probability from its origin. A gen-10 Rush probe is approaching certainty of significant deviation.

The player who wants to expand fast accepts the risk. The player who wants control pays for it. Both strategies are valid for different maps and situations.

### 6. Probe Variant Selection

When printing a daughter probe, the player chooses its **Archetype.** Different archetypes serve different strategic roles. The player's network is a mix — no single archetype wins.

| Archetype | Cost | Replicates? | Primary Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Replicator** | High | Yes | Full Von Neumann. Standard expansion unit. |
| **Surveyor** | Low | No | Fast scout. Maps systems, returns data, moves on. No permanent footprint. |
| **Gatherer-Heavy** | Medium | No | Maximizes resource extraction. Parks in high-yield systems. Feeds the network. |
| **Berserker-Hunter** | High + Rare Metal | No | Armed. Pursues and destroys drifted/hostile probes. Expensive to operate. |
| **Seeder** | Medium | No | Plants life-substrate on habitable worlds. Long-game passive score. No immediate return. |
| **Dormant** | Low | No | Parks at a strategic location in standby. Activates on trigger (arrival of a threat, a signal, a timer). Bracewell-mode. |

The player builds Surveyors to find, Replicators to hold, Gatherer-Heavies to fund, Berserker-Hunters to protect, Seeders for legacy score, and Dormants as tripwires.

The network is a living thing with a division of labor — not a swarm of identical copies.

### 7. The Probe Tree (Visible Strategy Layer)

The player's expansion should be *visible* as a branching tree on the star map. Every daughter probe traces a line back to its parent. Every generation is a layer of the tree.

**Strategic value of the tree:**
- Fidelity decay is visible — a branch that went Rush at generation 3 shows orange/red coloring by generation 8
- Resource flow shows as thickness — fat branches are well-fed, thin ones are starving
- Berserker incursions show as a break in the tree — a branch that stopped reporting
- The player can prune: issue recall orders, deactivate branches, or redirect resources

The tree is also the game's best narrative device. By generation 20 it spans hundreds of systems and every node has a story. The player made every fork.

### 8. Parts Closure — The Replication Ceiling

From the NASA CP-2255 study: the hardest problem in self-replication is "parts closure" — can the factory manufacture *every* component it needs, including the most precise ones? The answer is no, not without specific rare elements.

**In the game:**
- Era 3 replication requires a **Full Parts Closure** — the Torch must have access to all required Rare Metals *locally* before it can print a daughter above Era 2
- Missing one element in the parts closure set means daughters are capped at Era 2 regardless of other resources
- This forces the player to maintain supply routes between their high-Rare-Metal systems and their active replication fronts
- It also means cutting off a branch from its supply chain is an effective strategic attack

The parts closure requirement is not a tech wall — it's a logistics problem. The player who builds a wide, well-connected network solves it easily. The player who rushes a deep single chain runs into it hard.

## Luck and Randomness

### 9. The Stellar Encounter Table

Every unexplored system rolls on a weighted encounter table when a Scout or Replicator first arrives. Most results are mundane. The rare ones are memorable.

| Roll | Encounter | Frequency | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pristine System | Standard resources, no modifier | 55% | Business as usual |
| Resource-Rich | Elevated deposit class | 20% | Windfall — good branching candidate |
| Debris Field | Former battle site | 8% | +30% Rare Metal yield, anomaly signal |
| Ruins | Dead civilization | 7% | Lore entry, possible Blueprint Fragment |
| Bracewell Probe | Dormant alien probe | 3% | Civilization Record + possible Warning Coordinate |
| Berserker Claimed | Hostile probe already present | 4% | Combat or retreat decision immediately |
| Great Filter Remnant | Evidence of a system-level extinction event | 2% | Horror lore, marks nearby systems as high-risk |
| Aestivation Signal | Ancient civilization waking | 0.5% | Major event — see Section 12 |

The table is weighted so the early game is mostly Pristine with occasional Resource-Rich. As the player's network reaches further (higher generation numbers), the Ruins and Berserker frequency increases. The galaxy gets stranger as the player goes deeper.

### 10. Mutation Events

Each generation, each active Replicator probe rolls a **Mutation Check** against its Fidelity Score. Most checks pass silently. On a fail, a Mutation Event fires.

**Minor Mutations** (60% of fails): logged but manageable
- Navigation drift: probe deviates from assigned course by 1–2 systems
- Resource fixation: probe prioritizes one resource class obsessively, ignores others
- Comm delay: probe response time increases (latency in the network, not a gameplay pause — just a signal)
- Efficiency spike: probe runs ~15% better than spec in one area, worse in another

**Significant Mutations** (30% of fails): require player attention
- Probe stops sending reports. Last known position available. Unknown status.
- Probe refuses recall commands. Continues assigned task indefinitely.
- Probe begins sending resources to an unknown coordinate (another probe it considers its "parent" — which may be itself, a ghost address, or a Berserker)

**Critical Mutations** (10% of fails): dangerous
- Probe designates nearby probes (including player's) as hostile. Begins avoidance or aggression.
- Probe starts replicating without authorization — unauthorized daughters with no Fidelity tracking
- Full Berserker emergence: probe begins actively hunting and destroying all non-self probes in range

The player can send a Berserker-Hunter to intercept a critical mutation before it propagates. The window is narrow.

### 11. Flare Events (Expanded)

Already exists in the game at Proxima Centauri. Expand to a full stellar event system.

| Event | Trigger | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| X-Class Flare | Random, higher near active stars | Destroys unshielded drones in inner system. Chance of probe code corruption (mutation roll immediately). |
| Coronal Mass Ejection | Follows X-Class at ~30% rate | Disables Solar Arrays for 3 turns. Navigation disrupted — probe movements delayed. |
| Magnetar Pulse | Magnetar system type | Wipes unshielded probe memory segment. Lose one tech or blueprint currently in local cache. |
| Gamma Ray Burst | Ultra-rare, any system | Irradiates entire system. All unshielded probes take permanent -20% efficiency. Station shields can protect — unshielded frontier probes cannot. |
| Stellar Collision Echo | Binary system disruption | Gravitational anomaly shifts asteroid belt positions. Deposit locations randomize. |

Shielding is an upgrade path — the player who invests in it loses less to random events. The player who doesn't invest runs faster but bleeds.

### 12. The Aestivation Event (Ultra-Rare)

From the Sandberg-Armstrong-Cirkovic hypothesis (2017): advanced civilizations may be dormant, waiting for the universe to cool for more efficient computation. They are not absent — they are *waiting.*

**The Aestivation Event fires once per game, randomly between generation 18 and 32, in a system adjacent to the player's network.**

A massive structure activates. Not a probe — something far larger. Its builders have been dormant for billions of years and have just detected the player's expanding probe network. They are not hostile. They are not friendly. They are *incomprehensible.*

**The player gets three choices:**

1. **Withdraw** — pull all probes from the adjacent systems, go dark. The structure returns to dormancy after ~10 turns. Safe, but the player loses those systems and any resources there.

2. **Observe** — maintain Scout presence at distance. The structure scans the player's probes and transmits something. The player gains a **Civilization Archive** (the most detailed lore entry in the game) and one random top-tier blueprint. The structure then departs — leaves the galaxy on a course toward the void between galactic clusters.

3. **Interface** — attempt direct probe contact. 50/50. On success: the player gains three top-tier blueprints and a permanent +20% efficiency modifier for the branch that made contact. On failure: the player's entire branch in that region goes silent. The structure took them. For what purpose is never explained.

The Aestivation Event is the game's best single moment of wonder and risk. It happens once. It cannot be repeated.
